130 000 LITRES OF WATER NOW AVAILABLE IN DROUGHT AFFECTED THABO NCHU AND SURROUNDS

 

Global humanitarian aid organisation, World Vision South Africa has in partnership with Bloemwater and the Mangaung Metropolitan Municipality successfully installed 26 Jojo Water Tanks in Thaba Nchu and surrounds as part of its 150-tank/750 000 litres of water project funded by the FirstRand Foundation and other stakeholders. The project was headed by World Visions Thabo Nchu Area Development Programme (ADP).

 

The 26 tanks will provide 130 000 litres to community members residing in Thaba Nchu and surrounding villages and bring critical relief in an area which is still plagued by drought and resultant food shortage. The tanks will be filled regularly by Bloemwater and maintained by the various communities and the municipality.

 

In celebration of the project completion, two water tanks were handed over to the community of Middeldeel village situated outside Thaba Nchu. The ceremony was attended by members of the Mangaung Municipality, Bloemwater, Barolong traditional leaders, World Vision and the community.

 

During the ribbon-cutting ceremony, Bhekimpilo Khanye, integrated ministry director of World Vision SA accentuated the importance of partnership and ownership of the water tanks. These water tanks will cushion our children against the effects of the drought. It is therefore imperative that they have access to the tanks and it is filled with water and maintained.

 

He added the project would not have been completed on time without the hard work and commitment of the Thabo Nchu ADP and support of the Mangaung Municipality, Bloemwater and the Barolong council.

 

We put a lot of pressure on our ADP and they delivered. These water tanks are not ours but belong to the community and we hope it can bring immediate and much needed relief to the everyone, says Kanye.

 

The 5000-litre Jojo water tanks were constructed on ground level concrete slabs and met municipal engineering criteria during quality control and sign off of the installations.

 

The remaining 124 water tanks are currently being rolled out in KwaZulu/Natal (Ixopo and Umzimkhulu), and Limpopo (Thusalushaka, Kodomela and Giyani) provinces.

A group of 10 Emaswazini women in inland KwaZulu-Natal have established a successful bed & breakfast (B&B) business.

The B&B operates from a members home yard, and includes a string of mud huts built by the women. To construct them, we used readily available materials such as wood, stone, grass, branches, hides, and mud, using techniques passed down through generations, explains one of the women.

World Vision in Okhahlamba helped the women establish the B&B, and continues to equip them with business skills, advice on issues such as access to finance, achieving a work-life balance and working from home.

Already these huts, with no beds and only mattresses, have become holiday homes for many international visitors who enjoy the unusual experience, and being treated to a variety of Zulu dishes, as well as traditional music and dance.

Story Children do it for themselves in Umvoti

Children from villages in the Umvoti municipality in KwaZulu-Natal are playing a meaningful role in transforming their communities into better neighbourhoods. These teenagers are redefining child participation and have taken the transformational development concept to a whole new level. They have established their own egg-laying chicken project, started a childrens club called Soul Buddies and they monitor other children in their community.

14-year old Thabile explains, Our aunts from World Vision have shown so much interest in helping us succeed, making follow-ups on us at school and at home. We thought we could do the same for our peers. Thabile and her two siblings have directly benefitted from the World Vision sponsorship programme for the past few years.

Our main aim is to generate new ideas to solve problems in our society; we know we have the solutions! says 15-year old Umvoti childrens forum chairperson, Mqondisi Mthembu.

These teenagers have established an egg-laying project to grow their entrepreneurial skills. With 60 chickens, they have negotiated markets with the local shops and they are making enough profit to support needy children from the community with school uniforms and groceries .Children are a major human resource for development and key agents for social change and economic growth; what is happening here bears witness to that, said World Vision Programme Leader in Umvoti, Sayinile Mzolo.

Themes with c BEEP bikes are empowering children

BEEP, the life-changing Bicycle Education Empowerment Programme, was launched at the start of the 2014 financial year and by the end of the year, 6 000 bikes had been handed over to children in 58 schools in four provinces.

Partners in the programme are Qhubeka, responsible for donor engagement, donor of bicycles, spare parts and training of field mechanics; World Vision Switzerland, donor and funder of project implementation costs; and World Vision South Africa Project Management Services and World Vision South Africa Area Development Programmes

World Vision South Africa is responsible for setting up bicycle supervisory committees, mobilising communities, liaising with traditional leaders and local government officials, on-going monitoring of bicycle usage and adherence to the service-to-own contracts, data collection and analysis of monitoring and evaluation for impact assessment reports.

Of 16 million school children in South Africa, 12 million walk to school and of those, 500 000 walk more than two hours each way spending four hours getting to school and back daily. The BEEP bicycles are given to carefully selected children who desperately need transportation to attend school and obtain the education necessary to free them from a life of hardship and poverty.

Within a few months, BEEP monitoring and evaluation data found that:

         Daily attendance at school for Mbhashe and Umzimvubu ADPs improved from 35% to 82%

         The number of beneficiaries absent for one or two days in Umzimvubu and Mbhashe ADPs decreased from 47% to 15%

         Students arriving on time at school in Umzimvubu and Mbhashe improved from 17% to 81%

         Students completing homework every day in Umzimvubu and Mbhashe improved from 24% to 88%

         In Mbhashe, 72% of 337 students reported that their travel time to school decreased to less than an hour

         The number of beneficiaries who feel safe and very safe traveling to school increased from 131 to 307.

Simamnkeles life took a turn for the better when she became a beneficiary of a bicycle. She was waking at 4:45am every day to do her chores before starting her two-hour walk to Mchithwa Secondary School, a rural school in Idutywa, Eastern Cape, where she is in Grade 9.

It gets tough in winter. I usually do not have shoes and am forced to break the ice crystals barefoot, says Sima. She and her two younger siblings are being raised by her grandmother, together with seven other cousins. Without a doubt, this bike will go a long way to encourage Sima to fulfil her dream to become a social worker.

Ntswaki Mokoena, 15 years old, of Orange Farm, Gauteng, had been battling at school because of the four hours she spent walking to school and home again each day. School has been a challenge because sometimes when it rains, I would have to wait for the rain to stop before I can leave home, she said.

But that has changed and she became one of about 100 learners from Thamsanqa Secondary School in Orange Farm who benefited from BEEP.

Another 250 learners lives were changed from Hlumelo Secondary School in Mbekweni, Cape Town when they took delivery of their bikes that will enable them to easily cover the approximately six kilometres that they previously had to walk to get to school.

One of the beneficiaries, Nikho Nyaniso, was shaking with excitement. As much as I have always wished to have a bike, I knew my dream would never come true because my unemployed parents were never going to afford one, she said. Having a bicycle will not only help Nikho improve her studies, but will also shorten her day.

Fight against malnutrition continues in Nkonkobe

Agriculture is the main source of livelihood in Nkonkobe village in the Eastern Cape, but unemployment, poverty, and HIV and AIDS is rife in the area.

Over the years, World Vision has established and supports close on 30 community garden projects and 25 school gardens that together benefit the whole community. Sinesipho Caza, a Grade 8 learner at Debe Marele Primary School, is one of over 800 learners who has benefitted from the vegetable garden established at her school. Whenever we receive seedlings, we take good care of them and they grow to be real vegetables, says Sinesipho.

World Vision has also established a partnership with the Department of Social Development in Nkonkobe. The crop farmers have been empowered with skills for commercial farming and have received gardening equipment, seeds, fertilizer, and agro-chemicals with the proviso that some of the produce goes to surrounding schools.

By Andrew Newmarch, Senior Portfolio Advisor Southern Africa Team How long did it take you to get to school when you were a child? In the old days kids usually walked to school, but today, it seems that many students get dropped off by their parents in SUVs. In Australia, life has definitely changed. The thought of letting your child walk 4-5 kms to school on their own would be unheard of. In places like South Africa, this is the only way children can get to school to receive an education. But because of your child sponsorship, World Vision is changing this for thousands of children across the country.Take this High School in the Giyani project of north-east South Africa for instance. While in South Africa, I met Alive (yes, his real name!) He and his mates walk about four kms each way to school every day. Not only is this unsafe, it means that students are spending most of their time travelling when they could be doing their homework or playing with friends. However, for Alive, this is not a long way compared to other students at his school.In fact, in South Africa as a whole, 11 million of the 17 million school children walk to school, with 500,000 of them spending more than four hours a day getting to and from school.

An even bigger picture reveals that South Africa was placed 115th out of 144 countries with regard to childrens access to primary schools. Its no wonder that only 40% of children who enrol in Grade one achieve a qualification higher than Grade nine.

So what is World Vision doing?

We have partnered with Qhubeka, World Bicycle Reliefs program, to provide bikes to students across our child sponsorship projects.In the Giyani project where I visited last year, I participated in the unloading and registration of 490 bikes going to students, including sponsored children.

When it all started, bikes only went to girls to be able to get to school. It was about access and opportunity. But because of the success, a shift has occurred to provide bikes to boys as well based on distance from school.

Students undertake a contract to not only maintain the bike but to go to school part of the incentive is that after two years they can keep the bike if they attend a sufficient amount of school in that time. They are also given basic maintenance training as well as a, helmet, padlock and pump.

How do I know it will make a difference?

Based on two other projects, their results show that attendance has increased from 35% to 82%, homework completed each day has increased from 24% to 88% and travel time has decreased for 72% of the students with bicycles to less than an hour. Not only are fewer children missing important classes, the number of children who feel safe travelling to school has more than doubled.

But thats not all. The bicycles are helping out our staff too.

Singita is one of the Community Care Agents who recently received a bicycle from World Vision. Singita, along with other volunteers like herself work with World Vision to monitor the needs of community members and sponsored children. Before I had a bike, I used to visit only five families a day, but now I can cover up to 12 families in a day, she told me. Its great to see that the provision of a bike can transform the way the community operates and have such a significant impact on a childs safety and education. For those 490 students now on bicycles in the Giyani project, I have no doubt they are on their way to a better education. Since I visited the Giyani project last year, the program has worked so well in that another 1,200 bikes will be delivered there this year. In addition, another 900 have been promised to the two other World Vision Australia Sponsorship projects, Umzimkhulu and Ixopo, in 2016.
